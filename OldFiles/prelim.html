Question 1

Description: Abstractly, clustering is discovering groups of data points that "belong" together. More formally, clustering defines how to partition data. For this problem, you will consider data where each record/item is composed of multiple binary features.

Prompt: You should research, identify, and synthesize the key ideas related to clustering binary data. In particular, compare and contrast Bayesian and non-Bayesian approaches to the problem. Be sure to identify key approaches and algorithms for the problem.

References to get started:
[1] Nizar Bouguila (2010). On multivariate binary data clustering and feature weighting. Computational Statistics and Data Analysis: 120-134.
[2] John Lau and Peter Green (2007). Bayesian model-based clustering procedures. Journal of Computational and Graphical Statistics, 16(3): 526-558.
[3] Agostino Nobile and Alastair Fearnside (2007). Bayesian finite mixtures with an unknown number of components: The allocation sampler. Statistical Computing, 17: 147-162.

Possible simulation study: Compare and contrast two or more approaches for clustering binary data. Be sure to clearly identify the models and algorithms used. Also be sure to clearly identify appropriate metrics for comparison.

Question 3

Description: In many longitudinal studies, although the protocol specifies that subjects return for regular, pre-scheduled visits at which the response is to be measured, some subjects drop out of the study prematurely. One consequence is that the resulting the data are unbalanced; however, a more serious problem is that the reasons for drop-out may be related to the response. When the reason depends on responses that are observed prior to drop-out, the consequences may not be too severe, and this situation is referred to as "ignorable" or "missing at random."  However, if drop-out depends on the unobserved values of the response or other unobserved characteristics, e.g., random effects, standard analyses could be seriously compromised, and such drop-out is called "informative" or "nonignorable."

Prompt: You should research, identify, and synthesize the key developments and current themes in the analysis of longitudinal data in the presence of informative (nonignorable) drop-out.

References to get you started:
[1] Fitzmaurice, G. M. and Laird, N. M. (2000). Generalized linear mixture models for handling nonignorable dropouts in longitudinal studies. Biostatistics, 1, 141-156.
[2] Ibrahim, J.G., Chen, M.H., and Lipsitz, S.R. (2001). Missing response in generalised linear mixed models when the missing data mechanism is nonignorable.  Biometrika 88, 551-564.
[3] Little, R.J.A. (1995). Modeling the drop-out mechanism in repeated-measures studies.  Journal of the American Association, 90, 1112-1121.
[4] Scharfstein, D. O., Rotnitzky, A. and Robins, J. M. (1999). Adjusting for non-ignorable drop-out using semiparametric nonresponse models. Journal of the American Statistical Association, 94, 1096-1120.

Possible simulation study: Generate some longitudinal data with non-ignorable dropout and analyze using methods that do not account for dropout. Compare this with methods that account for the dropout mechanism.

Detailed Sample Prompt

Due Date: January 13, 2017 at midnight

Description: Sensitivity tests apply a range of stimulus values to experimental subjects and record binary responses in order to estimate the distribution of threshold values in the subject population, where thresholds delineate responses from non-responses. In many applications, individual tests are expensive and are conducted in small runs. The scarcity of data suggests that a Bayesian approach might be useful in the design of sensitivity tests. 

Prompt: Research, identify, and synthesize the recent key methodological developments in sensitivity testing. Please address, as appropriate, how Bayesian approaches, in particular the incorporation of prior knowledge, could impact sensitivity test design, how Bayesian and classical designs could be compared, and how a Bayesian approach addresses the estimation issues that arise when the data are completely separated.

Simulation Study: Based on your review of the literature, define and execute a simulation study. For example, your study could examine how the incorporation of prior information changes the test design and the comparison of Bayesian and classical designs.

References to get Started

[1] B. Never (1994). A D-Optimality-Based Sensitivity Test. Technometrics 36(1): 61-70.
[2] Y. Zhang and W. Meeker (2006). Bayesian Methods for Planning Accelerated Life Tests. Technometrics 48(1): 49-60.
[3] R. Etzioni and J. Kadane (1993). Optimal Experimental Design for Another’s Analysis. Journal of the American Statistical Association 88(424): 1404-1411.
[4] R. Tsutakawa (1972). Design of Experiments for Bioassay. Journal of the American Statistical Association 67(339): 584-590.

Additional Details

The review paper should introduce the topic area at a level understandable by anyone with a Ph.D. in statistics, but not necessarily an expert in the particular topic area. The student will need to identify, access, read, and assimilate the main developments in the area from the existing literature. Terms and notation that are not generally known by people outside the topic area should be defined. Key statistical issues and problems should be described. Discussion of major existing results and their evolution should be presented. Open questions for future research should be identified. A unified notation of the student’s choosing should be used consistently throughout. The paper should not be merely a compilation of facts and excerpts from the literature. It is acceptable to quote brief passages (appropriately cited) from published material when this is useful in making a point.

The simulation study should include
  • a clear description of the issue addressed by the simulation, why it is of interest, and how it relates to the review paper
  • a statement of the factors to be studied in the experiment and a description of the experimental design used to study those factors
  • a clear description of how the data were generated (with code included in an appendix)
  • a clear description of the choice of any parameters being used in the simulation
  • at least one table of results including appropriate measures of Monte Carlo variation
  • an interpretation of the results

Remember that a simulation study always includes replication (say 100, 1000, or 10,000 replicates) for each situation. It is not appropriate to just generate several data sets and analyze them. This is an illustration, not a simulation study. Since the results reported from the simulation study are statistics calculated from replicated data, some measure of the variability in the reported statistics is essential.

Each paper should include a title, the author’s name, an abstract, and a bibliography at the end. Choose a common statistics journal and adopt their style and reference guidelines.

The papers will likely contain some technical details and displayed equations. It is important to strike a balance between the extremes of no equations and too many equations. Mathematics should read like English. Use complete sentences, not sentence fragments. If a sentence ends with a formula, there should be a period. Do not expect the reader to fill in explanatory text between displayed equations. Do not use symbols instead of words in the text. Every notation, symbol, and acronym should be defined prior to its first use. Every sentence should begin with a proper English word and not a symbol or number. Text or formulas should not extend past the right margin.

The report must be typewritten and contain no typographical errors (spelling, duplicate words, poor grammar, punctuation mistakes). You must spell-check your document, but spell-checking alone is not enough. You must proofread your paper many times before submitting it. You may not have another person edit your paper for grammatical errors.
